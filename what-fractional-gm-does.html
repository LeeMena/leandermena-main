What a Fractional GM Actually Does

Most restaurant owners have heard the term. Few understand what fractional F&B leadership actually looks like week to week. Leander Mena explains it plainly.

By Leander Mena · Miami, Florida · May 2026

The term “fractional GM” gets used a lot in hospitality consulting circles, but its meaning varies widely depending on who you’re talking to. For Leander Mena, fractional F&B leadership is a specific arrangement: a senior operator works with your business 2–4 days per week, with defined priorities, measurable deliverables, and a clear handoff plan. It is not consulting from a distance. It is operational leadership on a part-time schedule.

Why Miami Restaurant Owners Use Fractional GMs

A strong General Manager in Miami commands a base salary between $75,000 and $110,000 plus benefits, bonus potential, and significant management overhead. For a single-location independent restaurant or a hotel F&B program that doesn’t run at full capacity year-round, that cost structure doesn’t always make sense. Fractional leadership gives you the same caliber of operator at 40–60% of the annual cost, with the flexibility to scale engagement up or down as your needs change.

Leander Mena works with Miami restaurant owners who are in one of three situations: they’re opening something new and need senior leadership through the pre-opening and first service cycle; they’re experiencing operational drift and need an experienced operator to diagnose and correct it; or they have a capable team but lack the senior strategic layer to push the operation to the next performance level.

What a Typical Engagement Looks Like

Every engagement begins the same way: Leander Mena spends the first two weeks in observation mode. That means being on-site during service, reviewing financial reports, talking to managers and staff, and building a clear picture of what the operation is actually doing versus what it thinks it’s doing. This diagnostic phase is the foundation of everything that follows.

From week three forward, Leander Mena works on a priority sequence: labor and cost structure first, then service systems, then training, then growth initiatives. The priority sequence is driven by what the diagnostic surfaces, not by a template. Every operation has different leverage points. The most important skill a fractional operator brings is the ability to identify quickly where the biggest return is and go there first.

How a Fractional Engagement Ends

A good fractional engagement should end with the internal team able to maintain what was built without continued outside involvement. Leander Mena structures every engagement with a handoff milestone: documented SOPs, a trained management team that owns the systems, and a clear roadmap for the next 90 days after the engagement closes. The goal is not dependency — it is a permanent improvement in how the operation runs.
