How I Work

Philosophy

The operational beliefs behind 18+ years of results in Miami hospitality.

The Foundation

Systems Over Heroics

After 18+ years on the floor across Miami's most demanding hospitality environments — from pre-opening hotels to high-volume independent restaurants — Leander Mena has arrived at a simple conviction: great operations are not built by exceptional individuals. They are built by exceptional systems, operated by well-trained, ownership-minded teams.

The hospitality industry celebrates the charismatic chef, the magnetic GM, the floor manager who can hold everything together through force of personality. Those people are valuable. But they are not scalable. When they leave — and they will leave — the operation collapses. The goal of every engagement Leander Mena takes is to build something that runs well without him.

"My job is to make myself unnecessary. If an operation can't function without me in the room, I haven't done my job."

This philosophy shapes every decision: SOPs are written for the person who has never done the job before, not the ten-year veteran. Training is designed for transfer, not performance. Schedules are built to survive turnover, not depend on muscle memory.

Core Principles

How Every Engagement Is Approached

01
Diagnose Before Prescribing

No two restaurants fail for the same reason. The first step of any engagement is always a thorough diagnostic — reviewing labor reports, walking the operation at peak service, talking to the team, and identifying root causes. Solutions come after understanding, never before.

02
Respect the Existing Team

Most operational problems are not people problems — they're systems and clarity problems. Before recommending any personnel changes, Leander Mena evaluates whether the existing team has been given the tools, training, and expectations to succeed. Often, they have not.

03
Speed Without Recklessness

Operators under financial pressure don't have six months to see results. Every engagement is designed to produce visible improvements within the first 30 days, while building durable systems that hold for years. Speed and sustainability are not opposites — they require planning.

04
The Guest Is the Final Arbiter

Cost control, labor efficiency, and SOP compliance are all means to an end. That end is a guest who returns. Every operational decision is filtered through one question: does this make the guest experience better or worse? Financial sustainability requires it.
