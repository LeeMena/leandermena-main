The Miami Pre-Opening Playbook

A 90-day framework for taking a Miami restaurant from empty shell to opening night — built from 18+ years of pre-opening experience across South Florida.

By Leander Mena · Miami, Florida · May 2026

Opening a restaurant in Miami is one of the most operationally intensive things a business owner can attempt. The city has a competitive labor market, demanding guests, high rents, and a hospitality culture that rewards execution and punishes chaos. After leading pre-opening builds at SLS Brickell, Maska Indian Kitchen + Bar, and other Miami properties, Leander Mena has developed a repeatable 90-day framework that separates successful Miami restaurant openings from the ones that never find their footing.

Days 90–60: Infrastructure Before People

Most pre-opening mistakes happen before a single employee is hired. The foundation is physical and operational infrastructure: vendor agreements, POS configuration, inventory tracking systems, and the first draft of your core SOPs. At this stage, Leander Mena typically spends the first two weeks in walkthrough mode — building a punch list of every operational bottleneck in the physical space before it becomes a problem during service.

Vendor selection in Miami requires more lead time than most markets. The best produce, protein, and beverage partners book fast, especially for high-profile openings. Leander Mena recommends finalizing your primary vendors by Day 75 at the latest, with backup suppliers confirmed for every critical category.

Days 60–30: Building the Team

Miami has a rich hospitality labor pool, but it moves fast. Leander Mena targets three hiring priorities in this window: the management team first, kitchen leadership second, and front-of-house team third. The management team needs 30 days minimum before opening to absorb the concept, contribute to SOP development, and begin their own training sequence.

Training in Miami requires cultural fluency. A significant portion of the labor force is bilingual, and your training materials, service scripts, and SOP documentation should reflect that. Leander Mena builds bilingual documentation into every pre-opening engagement as a standard practice — it improves retention and accelerates training timelines.

Days 30–7: Execution Rehearsal

The final 30 days before opening are where plans get tested against reality. Leander Mena structures this phase around three progressive rehearsal stages: internal tastings and menu walkthroughs, family-and-friends soft-open service, and a controlled preview service for a limited public audience. Each stage should surface problems before opening night — ticket times, plating inconsistencies, POS friction, service sequencing gaps. Every problem found here is one that won’t happen in front of a paying guest.

Opening Night and Beyond

Opening night is a data collection event. Leander Mena recommends staffing above capacity for the first two weeks — labor cost will be high, but the intelligence gathered during over-staffed service is invaluable for right-sizing the team permanently. Build a post-shift debrief habit from day one: a 10-minute structured conversation at the end of every service that captures what worked, what broke, and what needs to change.

The restaurants that sustain in Miami are the ones that use the first 90 days of operation as a continuation of training, not a relaxation from it. Leander Mena structures post-opening engagements around this principle — staying involved through the first service cycle to ensure the systems installed before opening actually hold under real operational pressure.
