The Miami Pre-Opening Playbook: What Most Concepts Get Wrong

By Leander Mena  ·  Miami, Florida

← Back to Insights

Miami opens more restaurants per capita than almost any city in the country — and closes them at roughly the same rate. Most failures are not caused by bad food or bad concepts. They are caused by the 90 days before opening, when critical decisions are made too fast, too cheap, or too late to fix before service begins.

Mistake 1: Hiring Based on Availability, Not Fit

Pre-opening hiring is always urgent. There is always pressure to fill positions fast. But the people you hire in this window set the culture of the operation for years. Hiring someone because they are available and willing is how you build a team you will spend the next 18 months managing around. The right hires take longer to find and are worth every day of that delay.

Mistake 2: Training the Menu, Not the Standard

Most pre-opening training is product-focused: learn the dishes, learn the drinks, pass the menu test. That is necessary but not sufficient. The hospitality standard — how guests are greeted, how problems are handled, how the team communicates during service — is what separates good restaurants from great ones. If you don’t train the standard before opening, you are training it in front of real guests, which costs you reviews you cannot undo.

Mistake 3: Signing Vendor Contracts Under Deadline Pressure

Pre-opening timelines create pressure to sign vendor agreements without full due diligence. In Miami, this most often shows up in produce, linen, and POS service contracts. Taking an extra week to negotiate terms, benchmark pricing, and confirm service level commitments is almost always worth the delay.

Mistake 4: No Soft Opening Strategy

A soft opening is not a favor to friends and family — it is a controlled stress test of your service system. It should be structured: defined covers, intentional pacing, a debrief after each session. Operators who treat the soft opening as a social event miss the most valuable feedback window they will ever have.

What a Structured Pre-Opening Looks Like

A well-run pre-opening has a daily task cadence from T-minus 90 to opening day. Every vendor confirmation, every training session, every walk-through, every staffing decision is on a timeline with an owner and a deadline. The operators who open smoothly are not lucky — they are organized. The playbook exists. The question is whether you have someone to run it.
